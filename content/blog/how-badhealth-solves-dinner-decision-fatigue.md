It happens to almost everyone. You get home after a long day, open the fridge, and just stare. There's food in there. Plenty of it. But nothing looks like a meal. So you close the fridge, open it again a few seconds later as if something will have changed, and eventually either order delivery or throw together something that doesn't really align with what you've been trying to eat.

This is decision fatigue. And when it comes to food, it's one of the biggest invisible barriers to eating well.

## The Problem We Couldn't Stop Thinking About

Decision fatigue is real and well-documented. By the time most people get home in the evening, they've already made hundreds of decisions throughout the day. Their capacity for good judgment is depleted. The mental effort required to look at a collection of raw ingredients and figure out what to cook — while also accounting for their nutrition goals — is genuinely hard.

And the healthy choice is almost always the harder choice. Ordering pizza is frictionless. Opening an app, picking a recipe, checking if you have the ingredients, going to the store for what you're missing, cooking for 45 minutes — that's a lot of steps for someone who just wants dinner.

We kept asking ourselves: what if that friction could disappear?

## The Idea: Let Your Fridge Tell You What to Cook

The concept behind BadHealth is simple. You open your fridge. You take a photo. The app scans what's in there, identifies your ingredients, and generates meal recommendations — not generic ones, but recommendations calibrated to your specific health goals.

Want to eat more protein? The app surfaces high-protein meals from what you have. Trying to eat low-carb? It filters for that. Managing dietary restrictions for someone in your family? It accounts for those too.

The key insight was this: the problem isn't that people don't know what healthy eating looks like. Most people have a rough sense of what they should be eating. The problem is the gap between knowing and doing. That gap, night after night, is where healthy intentions go to die.

## What BadHealth Actually Does

When you scan your fridge with BadHealth, a few things happen:

**First**, the app recognizes what ingredients you have. It doesn't require you to manually input anything — it reads your fridge the way you do, visually.

**Second**, it generates meal recommendations based on those ingredients. You won't see a recipe that requires four items you don't have. Every suggestion is something you can actually make right now, with what's in front of you.

**Third**, each recommendation comes with a full nutritional breakdown — calories, protein, fat, carbohydrates — and a brief explanation of how this meal supports your specific goals. You're not just getting a recipe. You're getting context.

**Fourth**, it learns. The more you use BadHealth, the better it understands your preferences, your household's needs, and the kinds of meals you actually enjoy making.

## Why This Matters Beyond Convenience

There's a bigger story here than just making dinner easier.

Food waste is a massive problem. The average family throws away roughly 30% of the food they buy — not because they want to, but because ingredients get forgotten, combinations don't occur to them, and things go bad before they figure out how to use them. BadHealth directly attacks this by surfacing what needs to be used and showing you how to use it.

Nutrition consistency is another. Eating well isn't about having one great week — it's about making reasonable choices most of the time. The nights when you give up and order delivery are the nights decision fatigue wins. BadHealth gives those nights a better ending.

And there's the personalization question. Generic nutrition advice doesn't work because everyone's body, goals, and life are different. A meal plan designed for the average person is designed for no one. BadHealth's recommendations are built around your goals, not someone else's.
