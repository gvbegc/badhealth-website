The internet has a very specific image of meal prep. It involves a Sunday afternoon, a dozen identical glass containers, perfectly portioned chicken breast, and a photo taken from directly above.

That version of meal prep works for some people. For most, it lasts about two weeks before the containers start collecting dust.

The problem isn't discipline. The problem is that most meal prep advice assumes you want to cook all your food for the week in a single marathon session. That's a big ask for someone who also has a job, a family, or any desire to do something else on the weekend.

There's a simpler version that actually sticks.

## why most meal prep fails

The standard approach treats meal prep like a production line. Cook five proteins, five sides, five vegetables. Package everything. Eat the same meals Monday through Friday.

This creates two problems. First, eating the same reheated chicken on Thursday that you cooked on Sunday is genuinely unpleasant. The texture changes. The flavor dulls. By day four, you're ordering takeout and feeling guilty about it.

Second, the time commitment is front-loaded in a way that feels unsustainable. Spending three to four hours cooking on a rest day doesn't feel like self-care. It feels like a second job.

The people who meal prep consistently over months and years tend to do something different. They prep ingredients, not full meals.
